Popular Vote vs Electoral College

  • Nobody had enough electoral votes to win. In turn, this invoked the Twelfth amendment because there was no clear result.
  • Under the Twelfth Amendment, any ties in the electoral college are to be broken by the House of Representatives among the top three candidates.

Corrupt Bargain

  • Since the voting in the House only takes into consideration the top three candidates, Henry Clay was out of the running.
  • Supposedly, John Quincy Adams had a meeting with Henry Clay saying that, if he endorsed him and gave him his support, he would make him his Secretary of State.
  • Henry Clay agreed since many Secretary of States became presidents later on in their career, so he could use this as a stepping stone in the grand scheme of things for him.

Or Did It?

  • The story says that there was some shady behind the curtain meeting, but nobody can actually prove it. So far, there is no such correspondence or documents that prove the very existence of this "bargain".
  • It must be taken into account that this story was spread by Jackson's supporters.
  • History may never know if this bargain actually happened or things just worked out for Andrew Jackson and he used it to his tactical advantage.

It is well known that Andrew Jackson's campaign for the Election of 1828 began the very day he lost in the contingent election of 1825. This corrupt bargain, regardless of its validity, had created a shadow of doubt on John Quincy Adams. It was perfect for Jackson to take advantage and realize his goal of becoming the United States president.
